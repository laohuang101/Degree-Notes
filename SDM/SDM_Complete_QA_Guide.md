### Question 2: What is SDLC? What happens when you DON'T USE SDLC in a project?

**Answer:**
- **SDLC Definition:** The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a structured, systematic framework used to plan, build, test, deploy, and maintain software or information systems. It is a process that consists of activities to build a system that requires a series of phases to plan, analyze, design, implement and secure.

**Consequences of NOT Using SDLC:**

| Aspect | With SDLC | Without SDLC |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| **Project Planning** | Clear timeline, defined scope, structured plan | No clear schedule, chaotic workflow, unclear scope |
| **Design Quality** | Strong architecture, security planning, stable database design | Weak or inconsistent design, leads to technical problems |
| **Time Management** | Predictable schedule with milestones | Frequent delays because tasks are not planned |
| **Quality Assurance** | Quality standards enforced throughout development | Quality depends on individual developers, inconsistent output |
| **Success Rate** | High chance of project success | High chance of failure, abandonment, or costly rework |

**Overall Impact:** When you don't use SDLC in a project as a guideline, there is an increased risk of failure in the project and poor quality of the outcome as there are no proper planning framework.

### Question 5: What are Information Systems (IS) Methodologies? Provide a few examples of IS Methodologies used today.

**Answer:**

**Definition:** Information Systems (IS) Methodologies are structured approaches or frameworks used to develop, implement, and manage information systems. They provide proven processes, techniques, steps, and rules on "what to do and what not to do" to complete a project successfully.

**Examples of Modern IS Methodologies:**

| Methodology | Where it is used |
|-------------|------------------|
| **Structural Systems Analysis and Design Methodology (SSDAM)** | Used for large database projects |
| **Web Information Systems Development Methodology** | Used for web-based projects |
| **Waterfall Methodology** | Used for projects with well-defined requirements and a clear scope where changes are minimal or unlikely during development process |
| **Spiral** | Used in large, high-risk and complex systems such as aerospace defense, and safety-critical softwares |
| **Rapid Application Development (RAD)** | Used for small/fast projects |
| **Extreme Programming (XP)** | Used for heavy coding projects |
| **Scrum** | For large and/or complex projects, it breaks them down into smaller and manageable "sprints", then distributes to the respective roles, allowing productivity to increase significantly |

## SDM Tutorial 2 - Structured Methodologies

### Question 1: Compare the differences between Traditional (structured) methodologies with new (agile) based methodologies of today.

**Answer:**

| Aspect | Traditional (structured) methodologies | Agile based methodologies |
|--------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------|

### Question 2: Why is Waterfall model called as such? Discuss Waterfall model and its significance?

**Answer:**

**Why it's called Waterfall:** The waterfall model is so named because the progress of a project flows steadily downwards through distinct, sequential phases, much like a waterfall cascading over a series of steps.

**Significance and Characteristics:**
- Provides high quality control of process and product
- Each step must be completed 'perfectly' before proceeding to the next step
- Provides high quality reports of each stage
- Easier to manage overall
- Emphasizes on complete documentation

### Question 3: Compare the ADVANTAGES and DISADVANTAGES of WATERFALL METHODS and V-MODEL.

**Answer:**

| Methodology | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------------|------------|---------------|
| **Waterfall Model** | - Follows a linear and sequential approach<br>- Each phase must be completed before moving on to the next<br>- Clarity helps teams understand their tasks and responsibilities<br>- Reduces confusion and enhances productivity | - Rigid and does not allow for changes once a phase is completed<br>- If requirements change or issues are discovered late in the process, it can be costly and time-consuming to revisit earlier phases |
| **V-Model** | - Has included early detection for defects in a system, allowing better quality product<br>- Keeps costs lower while maintaining quality<br>- Provides clear communication and management between development and testing phases | - Lack of flexibility and rigidity<br>- Difficult to adapt to changing requirements<br>- Limited customer involvement until later stages<br>- Working software is available only late in the development cycle<br>- Requires extensive upfront planning and documentation |

### Question 4: Do you think software developers of today still use these traditional Methodologies? Why (or why not)?

**Answer:**

**Yes, there are still software developers that use traditional methodologies.** Some reasons include:

1. **Familiarity:** Some software developers have familiarized themselves with traditional methods and are hesitant in changing their methodologies
2. **Clear Requirements:** Traditional methodologies like Waterfall method have requirements that are already clearly defined, fixed, and unlikely to change during the development lifecycle, adhering best for projects that need clarity of requirements
3. **Good for Beginners:** Waterfall is easy to follow and good for beginners working on small projects

**However, traditional methodologies are far less common now because:**

1. **Lack of Flexibility:** Modern software landscape is dynamic with customers constantly adding or removing requirements, making the need for change frequent
2. **Late Detection of Issues:** Traditional methods detect issues later in the flow due to how the process works, leading them to be strenuous and expensive to fix
3. **Not Suitable for Complex Projects:** For large projects, experts prefer agile-based methods

**Conclusion:** There are still software developers that use traditional methods depending on the requirements of the project itself as they have valid and valuable use cases in certain contexts where strict planning and predictability are vital.

### Question 1: Agile Principles Scenario - John (Game Developer)

**Scenario:** John is a game developer creating a game with 7 levels. He delivers the entire game after 7 months. The customer is not happy and has many comments and changes to do.

**Which Agile principle(s) that John violated?**

| Principle | Explanation of Violation | What John Should Do |
|-----------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **Principle 1 - Customer satisfaction by early and continuous delivery of valuable software** | He ignored it because he believed delivering the fully completed game would be better than releasing incomplete parts early | Instead of delivering the whole game only after 7 months, John should release small, playable parts earlier (e.g., Level 1 within the first few weeks). He should continuously provide valuable updates that customers can test and enjoy. Regular delivery ensures customers are satisfied throughout development—not only at the end. |
| **Principle 2 - Welcome changing requirements, even in late development** | He ignored it because he had done the development at the end of the 7 months which did not welcome change to the requirements in development | John should involve customers throughout the development process and collect feedback at each stage. If customers request new features or changes, he should update the next version instead of waiting until the end |
| **Principle 3 - Working software is delivered frequently (weeks rather than months)** | John waited 7 months to deliver the entire game at once instead of delivering it early and continuously (e.g., level by level) | Instead of waiting 7 months for a single final delivery, John should deliver a working build every few weeks (e.g., each level in separate iterations). Frequent releases allow early detection of issues and reduce the risk of major changes needed later |
| **Principle 7 - Working software is the principal measure of progress** | John measured the progress of the project on his own | John should measure progress based on completed, playable levels - not on internal progress documents or plans. Regular working builds prove that the project is moving forward effectively |

## Tutorial 4 - RAD and XP

### Question 1: METAGAME Scenario - RAD Application

**Scenario:** METAGAME is a new game developing company that has landed a project to develop a PC-based 'Children's educational game' for a local school. The game is set to have 10 levels which students will attempt progressively. METAGAME has hired a new team of developers who will be attempting this project for the first time. The project starts in January and is expected to be delivered to the school at the end of May (5 months). However, when the project goes into the second month (February), the customer requested that the project be delivered early on March instead of May (3 months). The senior developer has concluded that using RAD (Rapid Application Development) can be applied to solve the issues.

**How RAD can be used to solve the issues:**

| RAD Strategy | Example in METAGAME Project |
|--------------|-----------------------------|
| **Hiring experts with relevant experiences** | Hiring freelance game developers who can develop certain game mechanics with speedy deliveries and satisfactory results |
| **Skip unnecessary steps** | Reduce documentation requirements to focus on development |
| **Required daily face-to-face communication to obtain timely feedback** | Using online meetings instead of voice chats like WhatsApp when discussing the system with the school |
| **Use automated tools during testing** | Utilize artificial intelligence for debugging and ensure that the code works as intended |
| **Limit testing to prioritized requirements and functionality by the clients** | Only test the prioritized functionality by the school such as the system's operation |
| **Rapid Prototyping: Evolutionary Prototype** | Instead of documenting all 10 levels before coding, the team should immediately build a working prototype of Level 1. This allows the school (customer) to see the educational mechanics in action during February, identifying major design flaws before they propagate to other levels |
| **Component Reuse** | Since the team is new and time is short, they should avoid building game engines or assets from scratch. They can use existing game engines (e.g., Unity, Godot) and purchase or download pre-made 2D/3D asset packs suitable for a children's game to save weeks of art and coding time |
| **Timeboxing** | The remaining time until early March should be divided into strict timeboxes (e.g., 1-week sprints). If a specific level or feature isn't finished within its box, it is simplified or moved to a later update, ensuring that the project keeps moving forward to meet the hard deadline |
| **Joint Application Design (JAD) / User Involvement** | Require a representative from the local school (e.g., a teacher) to work closely with the dev team. They can provide immediate answers to questions about the educational content, preventing delays caused by waiting for feedback or having to redo work that was "educationally incorrect" |
| **MoSCoW Prioritization (Scope Negotiation)** | The team needs to agree with the customer on what is "Must Have" for March. For example, maybe delivering 5 highly polished levels is acceptable for the early launch, with the remaining 5 added as a patch later. This manages the reality of the shortened timeline |

### Question 2: METAGAME Scenario - XP Application

**Scenario:** METAGAME is a new game developing company that has landed a project to develop a PC-based 'Children's educational game' for a local school. The game is set to have 10 levels which students will attempt progressively. METAGAME has hired a new team of developers who will be attempting this project for the first time. The project starts in January and is expected to be delivered to the school at the end of May (5 months). The game involves lots of uncertainties; the development teams are often confused about what is required. The game also involves lots of complex algorithms to be used. The customer (school) insists that the game should not have any errors when delivered. The senior developer has concluded that using XP (Extreme Programming) can be applied to solve the issues.

**How XP can be used to solve the issues:**

| XP Strategy | Example in METAGAME Project |
|-------------|-----------------------------|
| **Test-Driven Development (Testing)** | Before creating a specific game level (e.g., Level 1), the team writes an automated test that defines success (e.g., "Student answers 5 questions correctly → Level complete"). The developers then write code only until this test passes. This guarantees the delivered game meets the zero-error standard from the start |
| **Small Releases (Short Development Cycles)** | Instead of trying to finish all 10 levels by May, the team plans to release 2 levels every month (a small release). This allows the school to verify the progress incrementally. If the team falls behind, they know in Month 1 rather than Month 5, allowing them to adjust the plan early |
| **Accept changing requirements at any time (weak point)** | Since the METAGAME team is confused and the requirements are uncertain, they might initially design Level 5 to be very hard. If the school plays Level 1 in February and decides the game is too difficult for the students, the team accepts this change immediately. They rewrite the plan for the remaining levels to be easier, ensuring the final game actually fits the students' needs rather than sticking to a "bad" initial plan |
| **Pair Programming** | METAGAME team can work together to solve difficult parts of programs together where one is the driver and the other will serve as an observer |
| **Continuous integration** | Whenever any developer completes a small task, they immediately merge the new code into the main codebase and run all TDD tests using automated tools. If a bug is found, the entire team immediately stops what they do and fix the bug first. This ensures that the game code remains runnable throughout the entire 5 months, preventing a large-scale integration failure just before the May delivery |
| **Pair Negotiation** | The METAGAME should include all parties including developers and stakeholders to negotiate any issues or approvals face to face to increase efficiency |
| **Stand Up Meeting** | Developer team are required to join to make sure the goals are aligned and to check if the team are facing any roadblocks (extra manpower needed, resources required) |
| **Hire expert** | Recruit developers that has expertise in the field to improve product delivery times and efficiency |
| **Simple Design** | Only implement what is currently needed, and do not pre-design features based on future guesses. They start Level 1 with simplest algorithms. Refactoring adds complexity (like adaptive difficulty) only when Level 4 requirements truly need it, preventing new teams from over-designing and wasting time |

### Question 3: Explain the process of RAD with example (and diagram) to create the METAGAME project.

**Answer:**

RAD (Rapid Application Development) is a software development methodology that focuses on speed, user involvement, prototyping, and continuous improvement.

Because METAGAME is working with a new team, has 5 months, and must satisfy school feedback, RAD is the perfect model.

**RAD Process for METAGAME Project:**

| Phase | Description | Example in METAGAME Project |
|-------|-------------|-----------------------------|
| **Analysis and Quick Design** | The first phase where the developer team and METAGAME need to determine the project's requirements. The requirement should be flexible enough so they can undergo any updates easily. For quick design, users (children or teachers at the local school) will interact with the system analysts of METAGAME and developer teams to give feedback, address shortcomings, clarify features, and assess user-friendliness | The team analyzes what the educational game needs for students, collects initial requirements from teachers about learning objectives, and creates a quick design that can be easily modified based on feedback |

It can include only the basic features, simple graphics and gameplay for learning that can reach the objectives or purposes of the game being created. | **Deployment** | Final testing of the completed software is tested to see if it is suitable for the students to use in school. Next, it requires providing training materials for teachers to guide their students later on how to attempt each level and the basic knowledge on the game's guidelines. Lastly, installing the game package into the school's PCs | The team performs final testing, prepares teacher training materials and user guides, and installs the game on school computers for students to use |

### Question 4: Explain the process of XP with example (and diagram) to create the METAGAME project.

**Answer:**

**XP Process for METAGAME Project:**

| Phase/Practice | Description | Example in METAGAME Project |
|----------------|-------------|-----------------------------|
| **Release Plan** | Discussing the goals and objectives with the school staff to develop the children's educational game in relation to the betterment of the learning process of the students in the long term, while also collecting the user stories from the teachers and students of the school | The team meets with school staff to discuss long-term learning objectives, collects user stories like "As a student, I want to answer quiz questions to test my knowledge" and "As a teacher, I want to track student progress" |
| **Iteration Plan** | Initiate the process of breaking down all collected users' stories (students and teachers) into programmable tasks, while also focusing on the users' stories with the highest priority, to establish the scope of an iteration, usually lasting 1 to 2 weeks | The team breaks user stories into tasks like "implement quiz question system," "create student progress tracking," and prioritizes quiz questions for the first 1-2 week iteration |
| **Acceptance Test Plan** | After each iteration, the school needs to review the game the team has produced. They must have tests being held at school for the teachers to verify its learning outcomes. Then the school must affirm if the iterations uphold the criteria with feedback, if needed some more improvement from the next iteration. Then it will be acceptable for monthly releases | After each iteration, teachers test the game to verify learning outcomes. They provide feedback and approval before accepting the iteration for monthly release |
| **Daily Stand-up meetings** | Each day the team must hold a short stand-up meeting to make sure the work is synchronized with the whole team. Each member must report during the meeting with completed tasks, planned improvements and the obstacles faced during the programming period. These are important to maintain communication and prevent delays | Every morning, the team meets for 15 minutes. Each member shares: "I completed the quiz question system," "I plan to work on scoring today," and "I'm stuck on the progress tracking feature" |
| **Pair Negotiation** | Highly collaborative discussion that happens during the Planning Phase (specifically, during Iteration Planning). It's driven by the need for the developers to understand what the project needs, and for the customer to understand the development cost. The negotiation ensures the team's time estimates are based on a shared, precise understanding of the feature, leading to more predictable completion dates for the 10 game levels | During iteration planning, developers and teachers negotiate: "The quiz system with 50 questions will take 3 days," "We need it by Friday for testing," and they agree on scope and timeline |
| **Unit Test** | This step is where we will ensure the foundational code is correct and error-free. The pair reviews the code they just wrote and simplifies the structure (e.g., changes variable names or removes duplication) without changing the outcome. The test is run again to ensure it still passes | After writing code for the quiz question system, the pair runs unit tests to verify each function works correctly, refactors variable names for clarity, and re-runs tests to ensure everything still passes |
| **Pair Programming** | One of the core practices of XP where two developers work together at one workstation: Driver who types the code, Navigator who reviews, checks logic, and looks ahead for problems. They switch roles frequently. It produces higher quality code, reduces bugs, encourages knowledge sharing and supports faster problem-solving | Two developers work together: Driver codes the logic for answering quiz questions and implements the scoring function, while Navigator checks if the scoring logic is correct and ensures the code follows game rules such as "10 correct answers needed to pass". After 20-30 minutes, they switch roles |
| **Coding Standards** | Happens after user stories are selected, pair programming begins and testing ideas are discussed. It is done in small increments, integrated frequently, and improved continuously. It keeps code simple and clean, integrate to the main project often, and replaces complicated solutions with simpler ones (XP simplicity rule) | The developers write small parts of code such as player movement and puzzle object interactions. They integrate immediately to avoid conflicts by pushing the code to the main project several times a day and refactor by simplifying similar codes by creating a reusable puzzle module. They run the game and check if the puzzle responds. If error occurs, they fix the issues immediately |

## Tutorial 5 - Socio Technical Methodology

### Question 1: What are the differences between a single IS methodology (like XP) and Software Development Framework?

**Answer:**

| Aspect | IS Methodology (e.g., XP, RAD) | Software Development Framework |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Definition** | A proven process or a "recipe" that contains specific techniques, steps, and rules on "what to do and what not to do" to complete a project | A template or a "form" with empty spaces that developers fill with relevant data (e.g., user needs, age, skills) to model a system |
| **Structure** | Has rigid or defined steps (e.g., Planning → Analysis → Design) | Provides a structure or boundaries to guide thinking and data collection, rather than a step-by-step process |
| **Includes** | Techniques (e.g., Pair programming) and Tools | - |
| **Relationship** | Methodologies can contain frameworks (e.g., The Agile methodology might use the CATWOE framework to gather requirements) | Frameworks are tools used within methodologies to organize information |

### Question 2: What types of systems would require you to study the various characteristics of the User? List and explain a few examples of System.

**Answer:**

**Types of systems requiring user study:** Any kind of system that doesn't have a fixed process.

**Categories:**
1. **High-Consequence / Safety-Critical Systems:** Where user error caused by poor design could lead to loss of life or massive financial loss
2. **Collaborative Systems (CSCW):** Systems designed to facilitate group work, where understanding group dynamics and communication styles is essential
3. **Public/Consumer-Facing Systems:** Systems used by a massive, diverse population with varying levels of digital literacy and accessibility needs
4. **Complex Decision Support Systems:** Systems used by experts (like doctors or traders) to make high-pressure decisions

**Examples:**

| System | Why study users? | Characteristics |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Hospital Information System (HIS) / Electronic Health Records (EHR)** | Users range from highly stressed doctors to administrative clerks. ### Question 3: What is the purpose of creating a PERSONA? How do you create a PERSONA? Explain your answers with examples.

**Answer:**

**Purpose of Persona:**
The purpose of a Persona is to create a model of a "perfect" or "average" user based on collected data. This model helps developers make specific design decisions (such as screen height, language, or menu layout) that mimic the user's needs and behaviors, ensuring the system caters to the majority of the target audience.

**How to create a Persona:**
You create a persona by studying the user demographics in the real world. You collect average data regarding their:
- Age
- Skills / Computer Literacy
- Physical Limitations
- Education / Literacy
- Language Preferences

**Example:**
- **Context:** Designing a McDonald's Self-Service Kiosk
- **Data Collected:** The average user is aged 16–25, prefers burgers, is tech-savvy (knows how to use touchscreens), and speaks English
- **Resulting Persona ("John"):** Based on "John," the developers designed the kiosk to be a specific height (reachable by an average adult), set the default language to English, and placed Burgers as the first menu item because that is what "John" prefers

### Question 5: Discuss the GENERAL PROCESS of modeling (converting) REAL WORLD data into VIRTUAL WORLD (Software).

**Answer:**

Modeling is the process of observing the real world, selecting the important information, simplifying it into structured data and rules, and representing it in software so a virtual system can behave like the real one.

**The Process:**

1. **Study Real-World Behavior:** Developers go into the real world (Brick and Mortar) to observe and study the users' actual behaviors and needs
2. **Capture as 'Tasks':** Convert these observed behaviors into specific 'tasks' or functions in the virtual world
3. **Link Tasks:** Connect several tasks together to form a complete process
4. **Build the IS:** Build the Information System (software) to satisfy this process
5. **Continuous Improvement:** Compare the built software back to the real world to see if it solves the problem, then continuously update the system with new and improved tasks based on this comparison

## Tutorial 6 - Scrum Methodology

### Question 1: Discuss how Scrum Methodology strategies can be applied to solve the following problems in a Software Development Project. Give examples.

**Answer:**

| Project Problem | Scrum Strategy (examples + techniques and tools) |
|-----------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| **1. User rejects the final product as it does not meet their requirements** | - **User Stories & Acceptance Criteria:** Instead of vague requirements, Scrum uses User Stories to capture the user's perspective (e.g., "As a user, I want... so that..."). Adding Acceptance Criteria clarifies exactly what conditions must be met for the story to be considered "done"<br>- **Sprint Review:** Regular reviews after each sprint allow users to inspect incremental deliverables and provide early feedback<br>- **Product Owner Collaboration:** The Product Owner acts as a bridge, helping translate non-technical needs into actionable backlog items |
| **2. When collecting requirements, the non-technical users find it difficult to express some of the functions that they want in the new system** | - **User Stories:** Scrum replaces technical requirement documents with simple, narrative User Stories. This allows non-technical users to express needs in their own language ("As a role, I want action, so that benefit"), reducing misinterpretation<br>- **Product Owner Collaboration:** The Product Owner acts as a bridge, helping translate non-technical needs into actionable backlog items |
| **3. Some of the processes in the projects such as problems and approval are delayed as the authorized people are not available to handle it** | - **Scrum Master (Servant Leader):** The Scrum Master's role includes removing impediments and protecting the team from distractions. They would actively work to facilitate these approvals or find alternative paths<br>- **Daily Stand-up:** Delays are flagged immediately in the daily meeting ("What is blocking me?"), allowing the team to address the bottleneck instantly |
| **4. The project sponsor brings in new requirements towards the end of the project** | - Bring in the new requirements by having the Product Owner add them into the product backlog while the Development Team continues to work on the current sprint without any changes<br>- As we bring the new requirements to the sprint backlog, the changes will be implemented in the next sprint<br>- This will maintain the efficiency of managing the workload while staying agile |
| **5. Project Manager does not have the technical expertise to address technical issues within the project** | - It is normal for Project Manager or Scrum Master to not have deep technical expertise because scrum separates managing processes from making technical decisions<br>- The development team owns all technical decisions and solutions (architecture, tools, and technical solutions)<br>- The Project Manager focuses on enabling the team to handle the problem<br>- Tools like GitHub create transparency and collaboration without the Project Manager needing to write code |
| **6. The project is very large, involving many processes to be carried out. The PM does not know how to allocate tasks equally among project teams** | - Scrum can manage large and complex projects by dividing it into multiple Scrum teams, each pulling tasks from their own Sprint Backlog<br>- Hold short meetings of discussion after a period to ensure balanced progress<br>- Example: In making an application, one team can work on the user interface while the other works on the database, each team selects their own tasks for the Sprint based on priority and skill<br>- **Workload Balance Rules:**<br>  - Estimate all work using story points<br>  - Calculate team capacity<br>  - Teams choose work during planning meetings<br>  - No team over 85% capacity<br>  - Distribute hard/easy work evenly<br>  - Consider team specialties |
| **7. Customers / end users are not always available for the developers to discuss their project progress** | - **Product Owner (PO) as customer representative:** The PO represents the voice of the customer. Even if end users are unavailable, the PO ensures requirements, priorities, and feedback are communicated clearly<br>- **Sprint Reviews:** Allow stakeholders to give feedback when available<br>- **Well-defined Product Backlog & User Stories:** Developers rely on these stories and review progress during Sprint Review meetings<br>- Example: If customers cannot attend daily discussions, the PO gathers feedback beforehand and writes clear user stories with acceptance criteria |
| **8. The developers keep making the same mistakes as in previous projects / processes** | - **Sprint Retrospectives:** Teams identify what went wrong and create action items to prevent repetition<br>- **Continuous Improvement (Inspect & Adapt):** Emphasis on learning from past mistakes<br>- **Definition of Done (DoD):** Ensures consistent quality standards<br>- **Use cross-functional team:** Example: If developers repeatedly forget unit testing, during the Retrospective the team agrees to add "Unit tests written and reviewed" to the Definition of Done and use a checklist before marking tasks complete |
| **9. The Product Owner allocates tasks to Team-A (Java developers) but does not have any task for Team-B (testers). This makes the time management inefficient** | - The Product Owner can insert the tasks in the "backlog" section within the Scrum Board. Teams A and B can choose what tasks they would like to do from the backlog<br>- Moving on, the team can combine a few tasks into a sprint, then bring forward to the "in progress" section of Scrum Board<br>- **Cross-functional team approach:** The development team could split among themselves and join the other teams. For example, if there are five members in Team B, two members should join Team A, so that after the developers finish developing the system, the testers who joined Team A can test the system simultaneously, speeding up the overall process |
| **10. Some of the software development tasks assigned to the developer could not be completed on time** | - **Return Unfinished Tasks to Product Backlog:** The developer should return the undone tasks after a sprint to the product backlog to be further reevaluated<br>- These unfinished tasks will then be prioritized by the product owner to determine the task's importance<br>- The developers can use this experience to make a much more calculated estimation of the approximate time for completion<br>- Not all unfinished tasks would be carried over to the next immediate sprint as the product owner could feel it is not a priority |

### Question 11: Discuss the challenges / setback / disadvantages of Scrum Methodology.

**Answer:**

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Scope Creep** | Because Scrum welcomes changing requirements and adds them to the Product Backlog, there is a risk of the project scope growing indefinitely if the Product Owner does not strictly manage priorities and trade-offs |
| **Role Dependency** | Success relies heavily on the Product Owner and Scrum Master roles being performed effectively. If the Product Owner is unavailable or indecisive, or if the Scrum Master fails to remove blockers, the team's progress stalls |
| **Meeting Overhead** | Scrum prescribes specific ceremonies (Sprint Planning, Daily Stand-up, Review, Retrospective). If not managed efficiently, these can feel like excessive meeting time, reducing actual development time |
| **Requires Team Maturity** | The Self-Organizing Team concept requires motivated, skilled, and disciplined individuals. Teams that lack experience or self-discipline may struggle to manage their own work and meet sprint goals without traditional top-down management |
| **Difficulty in scaling** | While "Scrum of Scrums" exists, coordinating multiple Scrum teams for very large, complex projects can be challenging compared to traditional hierarchical management structures |
